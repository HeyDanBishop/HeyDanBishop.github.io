CLIMATE POLICY RADAR - 2022

AI-powered climate policy research

Supporting better decision-making regarding climate action to help the transition to a low-carbon world.

My role

My role was to evolve CPR’s policy discovery tool from alpha to beta launch, incorporate experience design methods into their product development approach and mentor the team in conducting continuous user engagement.

Methods

User research, web analytics, usability testing, UI design

Team

Founder, Development team, Data Science, Policy & Domain experts


Outcome

  • By 2023 300,000+ users in over 100 countries use the platform
  • Can search through half a million pages of national climate laws, policies and UNFCCC submissions from national governments, translated into english
  • Established a user engagement framework appropriate for CPR's product development process
  • Cultivated a fledgling user community for user research and testing

Background

An initial alpha product had been created by a data science and development team proving that the technology could work. With limited user discoveries incorporated into the design process. Relying instead on first-hand experiences, collective assumptions amongst the team and best endeavours UI design work.

Establishing a user research strategy

Some team members had a broad research background and strong opinions but limited access to actual users to understand their context and obtain feedback. Relying on ad-hoc, limited access to peers in the wider community, with no formal user engagement process established.

Initial goals

  • Understand the type of work users do (e.g. a policymaker, an academic)
  • What questions are they trying to answer
  • How they work and where they encounter challenges
  • What could make the tools more useful to them

Discoveries

  • Users are typically knowledge workers who need to answer research questions as part of their jobs
  • Their roles includes civil servant policy analysts, NGO researchers, PhD students, risk modellers and scientific advisors
  • Problems encountered are limited time available to conduct research, having to consult multiple sources and manually extract data from long policy documents that are written in multiple languages
  • Preparing for key events, like the annual COP summit, hundreds of documents are released just before the event that need synthesising quickly

Created personas to provide focus

Many user types were identified, to provide focus I grouped each by similar needs and goals into 2 main personas: Researcher and Political Decision-Maker.
This overcame challenges encountered regarding:

  • Multiple user types to consider when designing
  • Mixed opinions amongst the team about who the users were and their needs
  • A stanrdard means of communication: internally amongst the project team and externally with partners



Key personas were identified as the main focus, covering the widest range of needs, behaviours and differing levels of engagement.



User journey mapping identified current experiences and provided a basis to collectively discuss and decide upon opportunities to improve the experience for each Persona.

Establishing a user community

Alpha users were encouraged to register for early access and then contacted about their experiences. After launch, everybody who gets in contact (i.e. submit law or policy documents, request data set download) is invited to join a user feedback session. And then invited to join a community, providing easy access to the user base to conduct research studies or to ask quick tactical questions.

Making research a team effort

As user engagement need to continue beyond my availability the product team would need to execute aspects of user research.
Key activities that helped:
Monitor interviewers performance to improve performance interviewers reviewed their sessions at the end. With session notes template included a checklist of things that the interviewer should avoid. Things like “don’t ask leading questions” and “don’t ask yes/no questions”.
Share findings every user conversation required a one-page summary including key insights and opportunities for improvement. Available for the whole team to view and learn from. Fortnightly team sessions shared learnings from user research with the whole team.

Ongoing journey management

User lifecycle identified

Identified current understanding of how the tool was being used, applying both persona’s context to a typical product usage lifecycle.


User Lifecycle identifies how the tool is used, highlighting where high-value actions are performed and drops in engagement.

Tracked in google analytics

Using a combination of standard out-the-box and customised tracking measurements

  • Engagement stage - looking at interaction with text passages matching search criteria, viewing documents or downloading the original document. Customised to measure interaction with physical document pages and variables on detail pages to understand how document types perform
  • Recurrence stage - how many users return and perform specific actions (i.e. searching, viewing document passages) on original and repeat visits. Assuming they’ve found enough value in the tool to return and use it again
