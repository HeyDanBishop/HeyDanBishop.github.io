CLARO WELLBEING - 2021

Financial coaching service launch

Claro's missions is to bridge the financial advice gap in the UK where 66% of UK adults don't have access to financial guidance and support. Financial Coaching is seen as an accessible alternative regardless of people’s situation.

My role

Establish an expereince vision, evolve a coaching product from MVP to launch, assemble a small design team and define an experience design process appropriate for an early stage start-up.

Methods

Value proposition development, service & product prototyping, user interviews, user testing, story mapping, mobile design, UI design

Team

Product Manager, Product Designers, Content Design, Growth Strategist


Outcome

Multi-channel service launch focused on delivering accessible financial coaching alongside saving, investing, education and budgeting features.

3 months after launch, the service achieved:
40,000+ users with an average app rating of 4.3/5
£2-3 million invested through the app into Claro's ESG funds
1.5k coaching sessions conducted

Scaled into B2B offering as a compensation benefit for employees to provide financial coaching, education and access to tools.

Longitudinal study of end-to-end journey

Capturing feedback from 20 beta users via a series of micro-surveys and one-to-one interviews, over a four week period.


Early customer journey an early version used as a reference to
build the end-to-end journey for beta.

Study findings

  • Some journey stages progress slowly most users needed time to set up financial goals after a coaching session. The assumption was this would typically be instantaneous whereas in reality this would be over many weeks
  • Users had low awareness of Claro products after coaching sessions, many users didn’t realise they could start financial goals with a Claro product. Even though products were associated with a sessions notes. Possibly due to coaches only being able to guide decisions but not decide for them
  • Knowledgeable users wanted to customise coaching sessions, as they already knew about some session topics. Wanting to focus on their priorities, or high-value topics such as investing and complete specific activities separately
  • More likely to progress if they’ve already identified a goal, users typically had higher engagement levels and were more likely to move quickly from an initial coaching session to start working towards that goal

POST LAUNCH OPTIMISATION

PROBLEM: Users not preparing for coaching sessions

Post-launch coaching session feedback identified that around a third of users weren’t fully preparing for coaching sessions, and some users weren’t even attending the sessions booked.
Impact:

  • Coaches can’t fully prepare and deliver a complete session plan
  • Customer value for coaching outcomes is lowered
  • Session rescheduling or no attendance impacts coach utilisation rates

Solution Hypothesis

If users can reach their first milestone of creating a financial plan that is relevant to them. They’ll be more likely to prepare and attend coaching sessions and act upon recommendations.

Journey update working canvas - included competitor design research, existing insights and relevant human behaviour assumptions to frame Ideas.


Concept to engage users through a long journey sequence

Focused on how to engage users through a long journey sequence that request lots of data and covers sensitive topics like personal finance.

Main ideas included:

Front-load coaching session preparation - moved to earlier in the journey, mostly before booking a coach.

Leverage internal triggers - using cues to prompt action based on emotions or existing habits.

Accommodate different starting points - help to identify financial goals, or if known start working through them.

Personalised financial plan and rewards - use the endowment effect to increase perceived value of a financial plan.


Comparative test

Remote unmoderated testing with 35 candidates across 2 prototypes was conducted to collect data on task performance, SUS (System Usability Score) and survey to capture user motivations and preferences.

Findings

Building a financial plan in a user-preferred order achieved more task completions. Choosing a financial goal that was very important provided motivation to also complete aspects of tthe journey perceived as less relevant.

Candidates earlier in their financial journey obtained more value from using the prototype and were also more engaged. These typically knew less about financial planning or hadn’t identified their goals yet.

Candidates were split across the rewards to receive by completing the financial plan.

The majority appreciated creating a financial plan required extensive data capture, but still felt that the journey was too long, with a recommendation to:

  • Shift attitudes from data capture steps to creating a plan
  • Decompose the journey into obvious chunks that aren't just about answering questions.

Design iteration

A further iteration of medium fidelity wireframes to incorporate testing discoveries.

Choose reward instead of selecting for them increasing motivation with relevancy.

Flexible goal-setting in a sequence appropriate to the individual - assuming key goal completion = task completion is more likely.

Timely messages about situations to create a sense of inclusion.

Change tone across the journey from ‘data capture’ to ‘getting a financial plan’.
